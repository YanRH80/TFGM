## Q01 — Representatividad de cohorte retrosprectiva
**Secciones informadas:** S1 (STROBE), S2 (Tabla 1)
**Estado:** ⬜ PENDIENTE

**PREGUNTA OE:**
```
In retrospective cohorts of immigrants diagnosed with Strongyloides stercoralis
and treated with ivermectin in non-endemic European countries, what are the
typical baseline characteristics (age, sex, geographic origin, immunosuppression
prevalence) and what are the main sources of selection bias to consider
when interpreting treatment outcomes?
```

**RESPUESTA:**
In retrospective cohorts of immigrants with *Strongyloides stercoralis* treated with ivermectin in non-endemic European countries, **typical patients are predominantly middle-aged (median age ~38-63 years), male (54-72%), originating from Latin America (particularly South America) and sub-Saharan Africa, with immunosuppression prevalence of approximately 6-7%**.[1][2][3]

**Baseline Characteristics**

The largest European cohort from Spain (+REDIVI network, n=1245) found that **66.9% were immigrants**, with **South America as the most frequent region of origin**, followed by sub-Saharan Africa.[1] A Barcelona substudy (n=865) showed more granular data: **67.6% from Latin America, 17.8% from sub-Saharan Africa, and 13.6% from Asia**.[3] The **median age ranged from 38.7 to 63 years** across European studies, with a **male predominance of 54-72%**. [1][4][3]

**Eosinophilia was present in 82.2%** of the Spanish cohort overall, though this was significantly lower (52.3%) in immunosuppressed patients.[1][2] Most patients were **asymptomatic or had nonspecific symptoms** at diagnosis, with screening strategies (rather than symptomatic presentation) accounting for 42.9-48.1% of diagnoses in recent cohorts.[3][5]

**Immunosuppression prevalence** was **6.4% in the Spanish cohort**, though this likely underestimates the true burden since many studies specifically excluded or separately analyzed immunocompromised patients.[2] In a Dutch tertiary hospital screening immunocompromised patients, **4.5% tested positive**.[6]

**Selection Biases**

Several critical selection biases affect interpretation of treatment outcomes in these retrospective cohorts:

**Diagnostic ascertainment bias**: Most European cohorts (78-90%) diagnosed strongyloidiasis by **serology alone** rather than stool microscopy, which may include false-positive cases and overestimate treatment efficacy when cure is assessed by the same serological methods.[1][4] Conversely, stool-based diagnosis (sensitivity ~22%) misses most infections, creating a bias toward more severe or higher-burden cases.[1][7]

**Follow-up bias**: Treatment outcomes could only be determined in **50% of treated patients** in the Auckland cohort, and similar incomplete follow-up characterizes most retrospective studies.[4] Patients lost to follow-up may differ systematically from those retained, particularly among migrant populations with housing or employment instability.

**Screening versus symptomatic presentation**: The shift toward **screening-detected cases (42.9-48.1%)** in recent years creates heterogeneity in disease severity and baseline parasite burden compared to earlier cohorts dominated by symptomatic presentations.[3][5] Screening-detected cases may have lower parasite loads and better treatment responses.

**Immunosuppression underrepresentation**: The **6.4% immunosuppression prevalence** is substantially lower than the[2] **41.3% found in U.S. hospitalized strongyloidiasis cases**, suggesting that European outpatient cohorts systematically underrepresent immunocompromised patients who may have different treatment responses and are at higher risk for hyperinfection.[8]

**Geographic and temporal confounding**: Reinfection cannot be definitively excluded in patients who return to endemic areas during follow-up, though this is less problematic in non-endemic European settings than in endemic regions. However, **long-term parasitological studies suggest that apparent "reactivation" (larvae detection months to years post-treatment) may reflect treatment failure rather than reinfection**, complicating outcome interpretation.[9]

**Treatment adherence**: Retrospective studies cannot assure adherence to multi-dose regimens, and only[7] **70% of diagnosed patients received ivermectin** in some cohorts, introducing both selection and information bias.[4]

Would you like me to explore the specific diagnostic methods and their performance characteristics in these European cohorts, or examine the comparative treatment outcomes between single-dose and multi-dose ivermectin regimens in non-endemic settings?

## Q02 — Epidemiología por sexo y origen geográfico
**Secciones informadas:** S3 (Demografía)
**Estado:** ⬜ PENDIENTE

**PREGUNTA OE:**
```
Does biological sex or geographic origin (Latin America vs Sub-Saharan Africa)
affect Strongyloides stercoralis seroprevalence, disease severity, or treatment
response in immigrant populations living in non-endemic European countries?
```

**RESPUESTA:**
***Geographic origin significantly affects *Strongyloides stercoralis* seroprevalence, with sub-Saharan African migrants showing higher rates (14.6-20%) compared to Latin American migrants (11.4-14%), while male sex is associated with higher infection rates but neither sex nor geographic origin has been shown to affect disease severity or treatment response in European immigrant cohorts.***[1][2][3]

**Seroprevalence by Geographic Origin**

Geographic origin demonstrates clear differences in seroprevalence among immigrant populations in Europe. A systematic review of migrants in Spain found **seroprevalence of 20% among sub-Saharan African migrants versus 14% among Latin American migrants**.[3] A global meta-analysis confirmed this pattern, showing **sub-Saharan Africa at 14.6%, Latin America and the Caribbean at 11.4%, and East Asia/Pacific at 17.3%**.[2] Within Africa, substantial regional variation exists: **East African migrants showed 42.9% prevalence, Central African 16.3%, and West African 10%** in a Spanish screening program, with East Africa conferring a significantly higher infection risk (OR 6.75) compared to West Africa.[4]

**Sex Differences in Seroprevalence**

Male sex is consistently associated with higher infection rates across multiple cohorts. **Male sex was identified as a risk factor for strongyloidiasis-associated hospitalization** in U.S. data, and European cohorts show **male predominance of 54-72%** among diagnosed cases.[5][1] However, the Auckland cohort showed an even more pronounced male predominance at **72%**.[6] This sex difference likely reflects differential occupational and environmental exposures rather than biological susceptibility, as males in endemic regions have greater soil contact through agricultural work.

**Disease Severity**

The available evidence does not demonstrate that sex or geographic origin independently influences disease severity or clinical manifestations in immigrant populations. Disease severity in strongyloidiasis is **primarily determined by immune status rather than demographic factors**.[7][8][9] The critical determinant of severe disease (hyperinfection syndrome and disseminated strongyloidiasis) is **immunosuppression, which carries a case fatality exceeding 60%**, regardless of patient sex or origin.[9][10] Clinical presentations in chronic infection—including diarrhea, abdominal pain, urticaria, and larva currens—appear similar across demographic groups, though **most infections remain asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic** regardless of origin.[7][9]

**Treatment Response**

No published evidence from European immigrant cohorts demonstrates differential ivermectin treatment response based on sex or geographic origin. **Ivermectin at 200 mcg/kg as a single dose shows high efficacy across populations**, with treatment success primarily dependent on immune status rather than demographic factors.[7][9][11] The landmark Strong Treat trial comparing single-dose versus multiple-dose ivermectin did not identify sex or geographic origin as modifiers of treatment response.[12][9] **Cure rates are consistently high (>90%) in immunocompetent patients** regardless of background, while immunocompromised patients require multiple doses.[7][11]

The lack of demonstrated differences in treatment response likely reflects ivermectin's mechanism of action—**binding to glutamate-gated chloride channels in the helminth**—which is independent of host demographic factors.[11] While **ivermectin resistance has been demonstrated in animal models**, there is no evidence of resistance in human *S. stercoralis* populations, and no data suggest differential resistance patterns by geographic origin.[9]

**Clinical Implications**

The higher seroprevalence in sub-Saharan African migrants (particularly East Africans) and male immigrants should inform **risk-stratified screening strategies** in European settings, but treatment approaches should remain standardized regardless of origin or sex.[4][3] The **6.4% immunosuppression prevalence** in European cohorts underrepresents the true clinical burden, as immunocompromised patients are at substantially higher risk for severe disease independent of their demographic characteristics.[13]

Would you like me to explore the specific diagnostic test performance characteristics (serology versus stool microscopy) in different immigrant populations, or examine the evidence for optimal screening strategies based on these seroprevalence differences?

## Q03 — Inmunosupresión y curación serológica
**Secciones informadas:** S4 (Perfil clínico)
**Estado:** ⬜ PENDIENTE

**PREGUNTA OE:**
```
In patients with Strongyloides stercoralis infection, does immunosuppression
(corticosteroids, HIV, chemotherapy, or biological therapy) independently
reduce the probability of achieving serological cure after ivermectin treatment,
and does it affect time to IgG seronegativization?
```

**RESPUESTA:**
***Current evidence does not demonstrate that immunosuppression independently reduces serological cure rates after ivermectin treatment for *Strongyloides stercoralis* infection, though therapeutic failures are more common in immunosuppressed patients and multiple doses are recommended rather than single-dose therapy.** Data on time to IgG seronegativization specifically comparing immunocompromised versus immunocompetent patients are limited.*[1][2][3]

**Serological Cure Rates and Immunosuppression**

The landmark Strong Treat trial, which assessed serological cure at 6 and 12 months, **excluded immunosuppressed patients by design**, limiting direct evidence on this question.[4] The trial found that **antibody concentrations tend to decrease over time in patients who are cured**, with similar proportions achieving clearance at 6 and 12 months (approximately 85-86% overall), but this was in immunocompetent patients only.[4] A Cochrane review identified two trials that included immunocompromised patients, but **these trials did not provide subgroup analyses for immunocompromised patients**, preventing definitive conclusions about differential cure rates.[5]

Expert consensus and FDA labeling indicate that **therapeutic failures are more common in immunosuppressed patients**, but this reflects clinical experience rather than controlled trial data comparing cure rates.[1] The FDA label specifically states that in immunocompromised patients (including HIV-infected individuals), **"repeated courses of therapy may be required" and "cure may not be achievable,"** with suppressive monthly therapy potentially needed.[6] However, these recommendations are based on limited data rather than rigorous comparative studies.

**HIV-Specific Data**

A small study of nine HIV-infected patients (five with AIDS) found that **all seven patients who received multiple doses (days 1, 2, 15, and 16) showed sustained clinical and parasitological cure**, whereas one of two patients receiving single-dose therapy relapsed fatally.[7] This suggests that **multiple-dose regimens can achieve cure in HIV-infected patients**, though the study was too small to determine if cure rates differ from immunocompetent patients. The Strong Treat trial design was influenced by this HIV study's finding of higher efficacy with multiple doses, though the trial ultimately excluded immunosuppressed patients.[4]

**Time to Seronegativization**

Evidence on time to IgG seronegativization is limited and does not specifically compare immunocompromised versus immunocompetent patients. In general populations, **antibody titers usually decrease within 6 months of therapy**, and **a proportion of individuals with positive serology may serorevert to negative (or have measurable reduction in titer) within 12-18 months following treatment.** The Strong Treat trial found that[1][8] **proportions of patients with clearance were similar at 6 and 12 months**, suggesting 6 months may be sufficient to judge response.[4]

However, a concerning long-term parasitological study found that **larvae were detected by conventional methods in 14 of 21 patients during follow-up**, with parasitological reactivation observed from 30 days post-treatment onward, and ***PCR detected *S. stercoralis* DNA in all patients** in both first and subsequent stool samples. This study, conducted in immunocompetent patients, suggests that *[9]*parasitological cure may be unlikely** and raises questions about whether serological negativization truly represents eradication versus suppression of parasite burden.*

**Immunosuppression Type-Specific Considerations**

**Corticosteroids** are the most specifically associated condition with triggering hyperinfection, along with HTLV-1 infection.[10][1] Among lymphoma patients screened for strongyloidiasis, **only 1 of 80 (1.25%) patients diagnosed and treated with ivermectin developed disseminated strongyloidiasis following chemotherapy**, supporting the efficacy of pre-treatment screening and prophylaxis.[11] In patients with cancer, **14 of 19 patients (74%) responded to thiabendazole therapy**, though two HSCT recipients with definite pulmonary hyperinfection developed fatal disease despite high-dose therapy.[12]

**Diagnostic Limitations in Immunocompromised Patients**

An important confounding factor is that **serology is less sensitive in immunocompromised patients**, with sensitivity declining to approximately 70% compared to >95% in immunocompetent hosts.[1] This reduced sensitivity makes it difficult to assess serological cure rates in immunosuppressed populations, as both false-negative baseline tests and impaired antibody responses post-treatment could affect measured outcomes.

**Clinical Implications**

While definitive comparative data are lacking, the consensus is clear: **immunosuppressed patients require multiple doses of ivermectin** (typically on days 1, 2, 15, and 16) rather than single-dose therapy.[2][6] For hyperinfection syndrome, **repeated doses are recommended up to 2 weeks after clearance of larvae from biological fluids**, with close monitoring.[2] The higher treatment intensity reflects both the increased risk of severe disease and clinical experience suggesting lower cure rates, even if formal comparative studies are absent.

Would you like me to explore the specific diagnostic challenges in monitoring treatment response in immunocompromised patients, or examine the evidence for alternative treatments like moxidectin in this population?

## Q04 — Régimen de ivermectina y tasa de curación
**Secciones informadas:** S4 (Perfil clínico)
**Estado:** ⬜ PENDIENTE

**PREGUNTA OE:**
```
In Strongyloides stercoralis infection, does the ivermectin dosing regimen
(single dose 200 µg/kg vs double dose on consecutive days vs repeated cycles)
affect parasitological and serological cure rates? Is there evidence for
a superior regimen in immunocompetent vs immunosuppressed patients?
```

**RESPUESTA:**
**In immunocompetent patients with non-disseminated strongyloidiasis, a single dose of ivermectin 200 µg/kg achieves serological cure rates of 86% at 12 months, which is equivalent to a four-dose regimen (days 1, 2, 15, 16) that achieved 85% cure.** Parasitological cure rates approach 100% for both regimens when assessed by stool testing. **For immunocompromised patients, multiple doses are recommended based on clinical experience and small studies, though high-quality comparative data are lacking.**[1][2][3]

**Comparative Efficacy Data in Immunocompetent Patients**

The landmark Strong Treat trial (n=309) directly compared single-dose versus four-dose ivermectin in immunocompetent patients residing in non-endemic European countries.[1] At 12 months, **serological cure was 86% (95% CI 79-91) with single-dose versus 85% (77-90) with four doses** (risk difference 1.48%, 95% CI -7.55 to 10.52; p=0.75).[1] When efficacy was assessed by parasitological criteria alone in the subgroup with positive stool tests at baseline, **cure rates were 100% (36/36) for single-dose and 98% (48/49) for four-dose regimens**.[1] The trial was terminated early for futility, as multiple doses showed no advantage and were less well tolerated.[1]

These findings are consistent with two previous smaller trials comparing one versus two doses of ivermectin (given either on consecutive days or 2 weeks apart), which found **no differences in efficacy** when assessed by stool tests, with cure rates close to 100% for both regimens.[1] A Cochrane meta-analysis confirmed that **taking a second dose of 200 µg/kg was not associated with higher cure** (RR 1.02, 95% CI 0.94-1.11; 94 participants, two trials).[2]

**Time to Serological Cure**

The Strong Treat trial found that **proportions of patients with clearance were similar at 6 and 12 months**, suggesting 6 months of follow-up may be sufficient to judge treatment response.[1] Antibody concentrations tend to decrease over time in cured patients, with **a proportion of individuals with positive serology seroreverting to negative (or having measurable reduction in titer) within 12-18 months following treatment**.[4] Eosinophil counts decreased rapidly from baseline, with more pronounced decline in patients achieving cure, though this cannot reliably predict treatment efficacy.[1]

**Regimens for Immunocompromised Patients**

Evidence for optimal dosing in immunocompromised patients is limited, as the Strong Treat trial **excluded immunosuppressed patients by design**.[1] The rationale for the four-dose regimen (days 1, 2, 15, 16) came from **a small study of nine HIV-infected patients** (five with AIDS) where all seven patients receiving multiple doses showed sustained cure, whereas one of two receiving single-dose therapy relapsed fatally.[5][1] However, this study was too small to establish definitive superiority.

Current expert recommendations for immunocompromised patients include:

**Chronic infection**: **Multiple doses are recommended** rather than single-dose therapy, though the optimal regimen is not definitively established.[6][7] The standard approach is ivermectin 200 µg/kg on days 1, 2, 15, and 16, or alternatively **200 µg/kg once daily for 2 days**.[3]

**Hyperinfection syndrome**: **Repeated doses should continue until stool or sputum samples are negative for 2 weeks**, with close monitoring and further dosing based on clinical review.[6][3] Subcutaneous ivermectin may be used when intestinal absorption is impaired.[6]

**Prophylaxis**: In immunocompromised patients at risk (e.g., before chemotherapy), the FDA label notes that **"repeated courses of therapy may be required" and "cure may not be achievable,"** with suppressive monthly therapy potentially needed.[8]

**Clinical Implications and Evidence Gaps**

For **immunocompetent patients with non-disseminated strongyloidiasis, a single dose of ivermectin 200 µg/kg should be the preferred regimen** based on equivalent efficacy, better tolerability, and greater feasibility.[1][4] The 2024 WHO guideline recommends single-dose ivermectin for mass drug administration in endemic settings.[4]

For **immunocompromised patients, the evidence base is substantially weaker**. The Cochrane review identified two trials that included immunocompromised patients but **did not provide subgroup analyses** for this population.[2] Multiple-dose regimens are recommended based on the higher risk of hyperinfection, clinical experience suggesting more frequent therapeutic failures, and the small HIV study showing benefit of multiple doses.[9][6][8] However, whether immunosuppression independently reduces serological cure rates when multiple doses are used remains unknown, as no adequately powered comparative studies exist.

The **diagnostic challenge of reduced serological sensitivity (~70%) in immunocompromised patients** further complicates assessment of cure rates in this population.[9]

**Secciones informadas:** S5 (Correlación)
**Estado:** ⬜ PENDIENTE

**PREGUNTA OE:**
```
In Strongyloides stercoralis infection, what is the expected correlation between
baseline IgG ELISA levels and peripheral blood eosinophil count? Do they
reflect independent or overlapping aspects of the immune response, and do
they provide independent prognostic information for predicting treatment response?
```

**RESPUESTA:**
***Baseline IgG ELISA levels and peripheral eosinophil counts show weak to moderate positive correlation in *Strongyloides stercoralis* infection, reflecting partially overlapping but mechanistically distinct immune responses.** Higher baseline eosinophil counts (>400 cells/μL) independently predict better serological cure rates, while IgG levels do not predict treatment response, suggesting these markers provide complementary rather than redundant prognostic information.*[1]

**Correlation Between IgG and Eosinophil Count**

The correlation between IgG antibody levels and eosinophil counts is **positive but modest**. In solid organ transplant patients, **seropositive patients had significantly higher absolute eosinophil counts than seronegative patients** (p=0.004), demonstrating that both markers tend to be elevated together.[2] A 2025 Iranian study found that **seropositive patients showed significantly higher absolute (1,473 vs 1,182 cells/μL) and relative eosinophil counts (18.97% vs 15.04%)** compared to seronegative individuals.[3] However, the relationship is far from absolute: in a Dutch cohort of immunocompromised patients, **only 41.2% (7/17) of seropositive patients had eosinophilia**, indicating substantial discordance.[4]

This incomplete correlation reflects the fact that **approximately 76.9% of infected patients have eosinophilia at diagnosis**, while IgG serology is positive in >95% of immunocompetent patients, suggesting serology is more consistently elevated than eosinophil counts.[5] The sensitivity of eosinophilia for detecting strongyloidiasis is therefore substantially lower than that of serology.

**Mechanistic Basis: Independent vs Overlapping Responses**

IgG antibody production and eosinophilia represent **partially overlapping but mechanistically distinct components of the type 2 immune response** to helminth infection. Partial immunity in chronic strongyloidiasis has been shown to correlate with ***eosinophilia, humoral IgM and IgG levels, and HLA-DRB1*0901 haplotype***, suggesting these are related but independent markers of immune activation.*[6]

Animal models demonstrate that **CD4+ T cells and ILC2 cells drive both IgG production and eosinophilia through IL-5 and IL-13 secretion**, but these effector mechanisms can be differentially regulated.[7][8] In primary *Strongyloides venezuelensis* infection, the hierarchy of importance is: **CD4 T cells/ILC2 cells > IgG and FcRγ > mast cells > IgE and FcεRI > basophils**, with eosinophils playing a role in parasite clearance but not being absolutely required.[7]

Importantly, **eosinophil activation (measured by granule proteins) correlates with intestinal inflammation**, while IgG levels reflect humoral immune memory.[2] In immunosuppressed patients, **IgG levels remain elevated even when eosinophilia is suppressed**, demonstrating that these responses can be dissociated under certain conditions.[9] A study of immunocompromised patients found that **hyperinfected patients differed significantly from non-hyperinfected patients in total and specific IgE levels and peripheral eosinophilia but not in specific IgG levels**, further supporting their independence.[9]

**Prognostic Value for Treatment Response**

The Strong Treat trial provides the most robust data on baseline predictors of serological cure. **Multivariate logistic regression identified three independent predictors of clearance at 12 months: younger age, eosinophil counts >400 cells/μL at baseline, and absence of skin rash.** Notably,[1] **baseline IgG antibody levels were not associated with treatment outcome**, indicating that eosinophil count provides independent prognostic information that IgG levels do not.[1]

The **median baseline eosinophil count was significantly different between those who achieved cure versus those who did not** (Kolmogorov-Smirnov p≤0.0003), but this difference disappeared by day 17 post-treatment (p=0.36).[1] This suggests that **baseline eosinophilia reflects a robust immune response that facilitates parasite clearance**, possibly through eosinophil-mediated cytotoxicity or enhancement of drug efficacy.

**Post-Treatment Dynamics**

After ivermectin treatment, **eosinophil counts decrease rapidly from baseline** (median 789 cells/μL) to day 17 (371 cells/μL) and further to 12 months (196 cells/μL), with the decline accentuated in patients achieving cure.[1] However, **it is not possible to predict treatment efficacy based on reduction in eosinophil count shortly after treatment**, as even patients without cure showed some eosinophil decline, possibly reflecting partial parasite suppression.[1]

IgG antibody titers **decrease more slowly, with seronegativization occurring over 6-12 months in cured patients**.[10] Serial serological monitoring is the preferred method for assessing cure, as **antibody titers usually decrease within 6 months of therapy**.[10] **Persistent eosinophilia 6 months after therapy suggests another etiology or therapeutic failure**, though eosinophilia may take several months to resolve even in successfully treated patients.[10]

Meta-analysis shows that **eosinophilia prevalence decreases from 76.9% at diagnosis to 27.4% after treatment**, though the association with parasitological cure is not clearly defined.[5] Concerningly, a long-term follow-up study found that **eosinophil values decreased initially (p=0.001) but were neither associated with nor predicted parasitological reactivations**, as larvae were detected in 14/21 patients during extended follow-up despite initial eosinophil normalization.[11]

**Clinical Implications**

IgG serology and eosinophil count provide **complementary diagnostic and prognostic information**. **Higher baseline eosinophil counts (>400 cells/μL) predict better treatment response**, possibly indicating a more robust immune system capable of supporting ivermectin's antiparasitic effects.[1] This finding suggests that **younger age and elevated eosinophil count indicate that a robust immune system is required for good response to treatment**.[1]

For monitoring treatment response, **serial IgG serology is more reliable than eosinophil counts**, as eosinophil normalization can occur without parasitological cure and does not reliably predict treatment failure in the short term.[1][11] However, **persistent eosinophilia beyond 6 months should prompt investigation for treatment failure or alternative diagnoses**.[10]

Would you like me to explore the specific role of IgE and IgG subclasses (particularly IgG4) in strongyloidiasis pathogenesis and their potential as alternative biomarkers for treatment monitoring?

**RESPUESTA:**
***After successful ivermectin treatment for *Strongyloides stercoralis*, IgG antibody titers decline progressively, with approximately 82-86% of patients achieving serological cure (defined as seronegativization or ≥50% titer reduction) by 6-12 months.** The proportions achieving cure are similar at 6 and 12 months, suggesting most serological response occurs within the first 6 months. **Complete seronegativization (negative serology) occurs in only 34-72% of patients by 6-12 months**, depending on the assay used.* Data beyond 12 months are limited, and median time to seronegativization has not been consistently reported across studies.[1][2][3]

**Serological Cure Rates at 6 and 12 Months**

The Strong Treat trial provides the most robust data on serological kinetics. At **6 months, 82-84% of patients achieved serological clearance** (defined as negative serology OR positive serology with ≥2-fold reduction in ELISA optical density or ≥2 titer decrease in IFAT).[1] At **12 months, 85-86% achieved clearance**, with no significant difference between timepoints.[1] Importantly, **96% of patients who achieved clearance at 6 months maintained that response at 12 months**, indicating that early serological response is highly predictive of sustained cure.[1]

The trial defined clearance as either complete seronegativization OR a significant titer decline (two-fold reduction in normalized optical density for ELISA, or decrease of two titers for IFAT), combined with negative stool tests.[1] This composite definition captures both patients who become seronegative and those with substantial titer reduction.

**Complete Seronegativization Rates**

Studies using stricter criteria (complete seronegativization only) report lower cure rates:

- A Spanish cohort found **34.4% (11/32) achieved complete seronegativization at 6 months**, while 81.3% (26/32) met broader response criteria (negative serology OR optical density ratio ≤0.6).[2]
- An Italian study comparing five serological assays found **72.5% seroreversion with NIE-ELISA** (the best-performing assay for complete negativization), while IVD-ELISA showed ~90% with "relevant decline" (OD halved or ≥2 titer decrease).[3]
- An Australian study of Cambodian immigrants found **65% (26/40) reached levels consistent with cure** and **47.5% (19/40) achieved OD [4]
- An Indigenous Australian cohort showed **83% (35/42) seroreversion after multiple courses of ivermectin**, 68% (13/19) after single-course ivermectin, at variable follow-up times.[5]

**Kinetics of Antibody Decline**

The pattern of IgG decline shows **most reduction occurring within the first 6-12 months**, with limited additional decline thereafter. A study using the Strongy Detect ELISA (SsIR/NIE recombinant antigens) found **~95% of samples showed reduction between pre- and post-treatment OD at 12 months, with a median relative reduction of 93.9%** (IQR 77.3-98.1%).[6] However, the same study noted that **few samples returned to levels below the cutoff for infection**, indicating that while titers decline substantially, complete seronegativization is not universal.[7]

The WHO guideline review states that **a proportion of individuals with positive serology may serorevert to negative (or have measurable reduction in titer) within 12-18 months following treatment**, suggesting this is the expected window for serological response.[8]

**Data Beyond 12 Months**

Evidence for serological kinetics beyond 12 months is sparse. The Australian immigrant study followed patients up to **30 months** and found continued antibody decline, with 65% reaching cure levels during follow-up, but did not report specific rates at 24 months.[4] The Strong Treat trial did not assess outcomes beyond 12 months.[1]

**Median Time to Seronegativization**

**No published cohort study has reported a median time to seronegativization.** The available data suggest that most serological response occurs by 6 months, with similar proportions achieving clearance at 6 and 12 months.[1] This indicates that **6 months of follow-up may be sufficient to judge treatment response**, though some patients may continue to show titer decline beyond this point.[1]

**Factors Affecting Serological Kinetics**

The Strong Treat trial identified that **younger age and baseline eosinophil counts >400 cells/μL independently predicted serological clearance**, suggesting that host immune factors influence the rate of antibody decline.[1] The dosing regimen (single vs. multiple doses) did not affect serological cure rates.[1]

**Assay-Specific Considerations**

Different serological assays show variable performance for post-treatment monitoring. The Italian comparative study found that **IVD-ELISA and IFAT had the best performance for detecting relevant decline (~87-90%)**, while **NIE-ELISA showed the highest complete seroreversion rate (72.5%)**.[3] This variability means that reported seronegativization rates depend heavily on the specific assay used, complicating cross-study comparisons.

**Clinical Implications**

For practical monitoring, **serological testing should be performed 6-12 months after treatment**.[4] A ≥50% reduction in titer or complete seronegativization indicates successful treatment.[2][3] Patients who do not show serological response by 12 months should be considered for retreatment, though it is important to note that **persistent seropositivity does not necessarily indicate treatment failure**, as antibodies may persist despite parasitological cure.[7]

**Secciones informadas:** S8 (Resumen eventos), S10 (KM sensibilidad)
**Estado:** ⬜ PENDIENTE

**PREGUNTA OE:**


**RESPUESTA:**
**The relative decline definition (≥50% reduction in optical density or ≥2-fold titer decrease) has the strongest evidence base and shows the best correlation with treatment response**, being used as the primary outcome in the largest randomized controlled trial (Strong Treat).[1] Complete seronegativization (absolute cutoff) is more specific but less sensitive, with only 34-72% of successfully treated patients achieving negative serology by 12 months.[2][3] **No studies have directly compared consecutive negative results to single-timepoint testing**, and this approach is not routinely used in clinical practice.

**Evidence for Relative Decline Definitions**

The Strong Treat trial defined clearance as **negative agar plate culture or PCR AND (negative serology OR positive serology with ≥2-fold reduction in normalized optical density for ELISA, or decrease of ≥2 titers for IFAT)**.[1] This composite definition achieved **85-86% clearance at 12 months** and showed that **96% of patients who achieved clearance at 6 months maintained that response at 12 months**, supporting its reliability.[1]

A comparative study of five serological assays found that **IVD-ELISA (90% relevant decline) and IFAT (87% relevant decline) had the best performance** when using the relative decline criterion (OD halved or ≥2 titer decrease).[2] The linear mixed-effects regression model confirmed a **trend toward seroreversion over time for all tests**, validating the use of relative decline as a marker of treatment response.[2]

A Spanish cohort used a stricter relative decline threshold (OD ratio ≤0.6, equivalent to ≥40% reduction) and found **81.3% (26/32) met response criteria at 6 months**, compared to only 34.4% achieving complete seronegativization.[3]

**Evidence for Absolute Cutoff (Complete Seronegativization)**

Complete seronegativization rates vary substantially by assay:

- **NIE-ELISA**: 72.5% seroreversion (best for complete negativization)[2]
- **IVD-ELISA**: ~50% complete negativization[2]
- **Strongy Detect (SsIR/NIE)**: Few samples returned below cutoff despite 93.9% median relative OD reduction[5][4]

The Strongy Detect validation study explicitly noted that **"although ELISA values did decline post-treatment, few returned to levels below the cutoff for infection"** and **"the assay remains positive for greater than a year post-treatment"**, indicating that absolute cutoffs may be too stringent.[4]

**Correlation with Parasitological Cure**

**No serological definition has been rigorously validated against true parasitological cure**, which is itself difficult to establish. The traditional definition of cure (absence of larvae by parasitological methods at 1 year) is problematic because stool tests have low sensitivity.[6][1]

A concerning long-term follow-up study found that **larvae were detected in 14/21 patients during extended follow-up (up to 4 years)**, and **S. stercoralis DNA was detected by PCR in all patients**, suggesting that parasitological cure may be rare regardless of serological response.[6] This raises questions about whether any serological definition truly correlates with complete parasite eradication.

The Strong Treat trial acknowledged this limitation: **"Negative faecal tests cannot reliably rule out S. stercoralis infection, so the efficacy of an intervention tends to be overestimated when assessed by these methods only."**[1]

**Consecutive Negative Results**

**No published studies have evaluated requiring two consecutive negative serological results** for defining cure. This approach is used in hyperinfection syndrome (stool/sputum negative for 2 weeks), but has not been applied to serological monitoring.[7] The Strong Treat trial found that **clearance rates were similar at 6 and 12 months**, suggesting that a single timepoint at 6-12 months may be sufficient without requiring consecutive testing.[1]

**Assay-Specific Considerations**

The choice of cure definition should account for assay characteristics:

- **Crude antigen ELISAs** (IVD, Bordier): Better for detecting relative decline; higher cross-reactivity
- **Recombinant antigen ELISAs** (NIE, SsIR/NIE): Higher specificity; better for complete seronegativization
- **IFAT**: Good for relative decline (titer-based); labor-intensive

A 2024 study of the SsIR/NIE ELISA found **~95% of samples showed OD reduction at 12 months with median relative reduction of 93.9%**, but noted that **"the threshold for positivity has not been predefined"**, highlighting the need for standardized cutoffs.[5]

**Clinical Recommendations**

Based on available evidence, **the relative decline definition (≥50% OD reduction or ≥2-fold titer decrease) combined with negative stool tests represents the most practical and best-validated approach**.[1][2] This definition:

1. Captures the majority of successfully treated patients (82-86%)
2. Has been validated in the largest RCT
3. Accounts for the slow kinetics of antibody decay
4. Is reproducible across different assay platforms

Complete seronegativization should be considered the **"cure" endpoint** when achievable, but **persistent low-level seropositivity with significant titer decline should not be interpreted as treatment failure** in the absence of positive stool tests.[2][3]

In schistosomiasis, antibodies against crude antigens **"remained elevated long after treatment"** compared to antibodies against specific recombinant antigens that declined rapidly. **The Strong Treat Multivariate Model**

The Strong Treat trial fitted a multivariate logistic regression model including "all clinical and demographical variables" to predict treatment response at 12 months.[1] The final model identified three independent predictors of clearance:

1. **Younger age**
2. **Baseline eosinophil count >400 cells/μL**
3. **Absence of skin rash**[1]

Critically, the trial **excluded immunosuppressed patients by design** ("We excluded individuals with immunosuppression because they are at higher risk than people who are immunocompetent of developing disseminated infection, which could affect the response to treatment").[1] Therefore, immunosuppression could not be evaluated as a covariate.

The published analysis does not clarify whether baseline optical density was included in the initial model and dropped during variable selection, or whether it was never considered.[1]

**Biological Plausibility of Eosinophilia as an Independent Predictor**

The finding that higher baseline eosinophilia predicts better treatment response is biologically plausible and likely reflects a distinct immune mechanism from antibody levels:

- **Eosinophils are directly helminthotoxic**: They deposit cationic proteins (major basic protein, eosinophil cationic protein) and generate reactive oxygen species that kill parasitic larvae. This effector function is antibody-independent in some contexts, though IgG and IgE can enhance eosinophil binding to parasites.[2][3]
- **Eosinophilia reflects active type 2 immunity**: A robust eosinophilic response indicates intact IL-5 and IL-13 signaling, which may be necessary for optimal ivermectin efficacy. The authors interpreted this finding as suggesting that **"a robust immune system is required for a good response to treatment."**[1]
- **IgG and eosinophils may reflect different aspects of infection**: IgG levels correlate with antigen exposure and B-cell memory, while eosinophilia reflects ongoing tissue-phase immune activation. These could provide independent prognostic information.

**Eosinophilia as a Diagnostic vs. Prognostic Marker**

While eosinophilia predicts treatment response, it has **poor sensitivity for diagnosis**. This dissociation between diagnostic and prognostic utility suggests that eosinophilia identifies a subset of patients with more active immune responses who are more likely to clear infection with treatment, rather than simply reflecting infection intensity.

***No published study has parametrically modeled the rate of IgG ELISA decline after ivermectin treatment for *Strongyloides stercoralis* in a way that reports an average monthly decline rate.** The only formal modeling approach used a **linear mixed-effects regression** that identified age and time as independent predictors of serological test values, demonstrating a trend toward seroreversion over time—but **specific coefficients (e.g., OD units/month) were not reported**, and baseline IgG level, treatment regimen, and immunosuppression status were not evaluated as modifiers of decline rate.*[1]

The key study evaluating serological decline kinetics used a linear mixed-effects regression model to analyze five serological assays (IVD-ELISA, Bordier-ELISA, NIE-ELISA, NIE-LIPS, and IFAT) in patients followed 3-12 months after treatment.[1] The model structure was:

- **Result**: "A trend to seroreversion over time for all tests"

**Factors Not Evaluated in Decline Rate Models**

The critical evidence gaps include:

1. **Baseline IgG level**: Not included as a predictor in the linear mixed-effects model. Theoretically, patients with higher baseline OD would require longer to reach the negativity threshold even if the absolute decline rate were identical. No study has tested whether baseline OD affects the *rate* of decline (slope) versus simply the *time to threshold crossing*.[1]
2. **Treatment regimen**: The Strong Treat trial found that single-dose and four-dose regimens produced equivalent clearance rates at 6 and 12 months, suggesting treatment intensity does not affect decline kinetics. However, this was not formally modeled as a covariate in a decline rate analysis.[5]
3. **Immunosuppression status**: No human data exist on IgG decline rates in immunosuppressed patients with strongyloidiasis. Experimental data in rats suggest that corticosteroids suppress antibody *levels* but may not impair antibody *maturation* (avidity), with prednisolone-treated animals showing increased avidity indices despite lower titers. This suggests immunosuppression may affect the magnitude of the antibody response more than its kinetics.[6]

**Comparison with Other Parasitic Infections**

Mathematical modeling of antibody decay has been more extensively studied in other parasitic infections, providing a framework for understanding strongyloidiasis:

- **Malaria**: Antibody responses can be modeled using populations of short-lived antibody-secreting cells (ASCs; half-life 2-10 days) and long-lived ASCs (half-life 1.8-3.7 years). The proportion of long-lived ASCs increases with repeated exposure (10% in primary infection vs. 10-56% in previously exposed individuals).[7][8]
- **Chagas disease**: Multiplex serological approaches have been developed to predict seroreversion, using a 30% signal reduction threshold in at least half of reactive antigens to predict eventual negativization. This approach accounts for the slow antibody dynamics in adults (decades to serorevert).[9]
- **Leptospirosis**: Exponential decay modeling estimated a decay constant of **0.926 titer dilutions per month**, which was used to correct for interval-censored antibody decline in longitudinal studies.[10]

**Clinical Implications**

Given the absence of parametric decline rate data for strongyloidiasis:

1. **Relative decline criteria may be more practical** than absolute thresholds, as they account for individual variation in baseline levels. The commonly used criterion of ≥50% OD reduction or OD ratio ≤0.6 appears to perform well across studies.[4][1]
2. **Follow-up timing**: The Strong Treat trial found that clearance proportions were similar at 6 and 12 months, suggesting that most of the meaningful decline occurs within the first 6 months.[5]
3. **Assay selection matters**: Different assays show different decline characteristics—IVD-ELISA shows the best performance for detecting relevant decline (~90%), while NIE-ELISA shows the best performance for complete negativization (72.5%).[1]

3. **Eosinophil count has normalized** in conjunction with serological decline

- **Declining antibodies indicate treatment response**: The IVD-ELISA showed ~90% of samples with relevant decline (OD halved), even when complete negativization was not achieved.[5]

**Distinguishing True Relapse from Reinfection or Serological Fluctuation**

In non-endemic settings, the distinction between relapse and reinfection is theoretically straightforward but practically challenging:

**Reinfection is essentially excluded** in patients living in non-endemic European countries without travel to endemic areas, as *S. stercoralis* requires specific soil conditions for transmission. The autoinfective lifecycle means that any recurrence in these patients represents **persistence of the original infection** rather than new acquisition.[2][1]

- Antibody levels can fluctuate around the positivity threshold, leading to apparent "relapse" that is actually measurement variability
- The Strong Treat trial defined clearance as negative serology OR ≥50% OD reduction, which may capture some patients with declining but fluctuating titers[1]
- Different assays show different patterns—the Strongy Detect assay "remains positive for greater than a year post-treatment" even when ELISA values decline, suggesting that some "relapses" may reflect assay sensitivity differences[3]

**Proposed Mechanisms for Apparent Relapse**

1. **Incomplete parasite eradication**: Ivermectin may suppress but not eliminate the autoinfective cycle. Larvae in tissue compartments (lungs, perianal skin) may survive treatment and re-establish intestinal infection.[4][5]
2. **Serological memory without active infection**: Long-lived plasma cells can maintain antibody production after parasite clearance, and fluctuations in this memory response could cause apparent serological relapse without true reinfection.
3. **Assay variability**: Inter-assay and intra-assay variability near the positivity threshold can produce discordant results over time.

**Assay-Specific Seronegativization Characteristics**

**Crude antigen ELISAs (IVD, Bordier, DRG)**: These assays use native *S. stercoralis* larval antigens and show good sensitivity for detecting OD decline but slower complete seronegativization. The IVD-ELISA demonstrated 92.3% sensitivity and 97.4% specificity for diagnosis, with ~90% of treated patients showing relevant decline at 3-12 months.[1][3] However, a recent evaluation found lower sensitivity (58-64%) in travelers with acute/recent infections, suggesting performance varies by infection chronicity.[4]

**Recombinant antigen ELISAs (NIE-ELISA, Strongy Detect)**: These assays use recombinant NIE and/or SsIR antigens and show different kinetics:

- **NIE-ELISA**: Achieves the highest rate of complete seronegativization (72.5%) but has lower sensitivity for initial diagnosis (72-79%).[1][5]
- **Strongy Detect (SsIR/NIE)**: Shows excellent diagnostic accuracy (99% sensitivity, 99% specificity) but **"remains positive for greater than a year post-treatment"** despite declining OD values. The median relative OD reduction was 93.9% (IQR 77.3%–98.1%) at 12 months, but few samples crossed below the positivity threshold.[6][2]

**IgG4-based assays**: The IgG4 Strongy Detect showed 96% sensitivity and 100% specificity for diagnosis, but post-treatment dynamics have not been specifically characterized.[2]

**Clinical Implications of Assay Selection**

1. **For treatment monitoring, use the same assay at baseline and follow-up**: The relative decline (OD ratio post/pre ≤0.6) is more reliable than absolute thresholds when comparing within-patient changes.[1][9]
2. **Crude antigen ELISAs (IVD, Bordier) are preferred for detecting treatment response**: They show the highest proportion of samples with relevant decline (~90%), making them suitable for confirming that treatment had an effect.[1]
3. **Recombinant antigen ELISAs (NIE-ELISA) may be preferred for confirming cure**: The higher seronegativization rate (72.5%) makes them better suited for determining when follow-up can be discontinued.[1]
4. **Strongy Detect has a technical limitation for follow-up**: The lack of a predefined positivity threshold and overflow signals at high OD values complicate its use for monitoring, despite excellent diagnostic accuracy.[6]
5. **Rapid tests (SsRapid) show promise but limited follow-up data**: The IgG4 rapid test showed substantial agreement with ELISA (κ = 0.776) for diagnosis, but post-treatment kinetics are not well characterized.[10][11]

**Assay-Specific Timeline Differences**

While no study has directly compared seronegativization timelines across assays in the same patients, the available data suggest:

- **IVD-ELISA**: Most decline occurs by 6 months; 6-month and 12-month clearance rates are similar[9]
- **NIE-ELISA**: Higher proportion achieve complete negativization, but timeline similar to crude antigen assays[1]
- **Strongy Detect**: Remains positive >1 year despite declining values; may require longer follow-up or different interpretation criteria[2]
- **IFAT**: Shows good decline (~87%) but requires specialized laboratory capacity[1]

The WHO review acknowledges this heterogeneity, noting that serological tests have **"larger variation between assays"** and that **"a proportion of individuals with a positive serological test might serorevert to negative (or have a measurable reduction in titre) within 12–18 months following treatment."**[12]
